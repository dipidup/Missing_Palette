Banaras is not a place you simply visit. It's something you absorb.

The ghats were alive long before sunrise. Priests preparing rituals. People sitting quietly by the river. Boats moving slowly through the mist. Everything felt both chaotic and peaceful at the same time. I remember sitting with my sketchbook, trying to decide where to begin. There was too much happening. Too many stories in a single frame.

That's when I realized - I didn't need to capture *everything*. I just needed to capture a *feeling*.

![<span class="hi">बनारसी शाम</span> · Banarasi Shaam - the Ganga at evening, painted from a step on the river.](/img/gallery/water (14).jpg)

## Finding a *moment* in the chaos

Instead of focusing on every detail, I chose a small section - a cluster of steps, a few figures, the soft reflection of light on water. The rest could remain suggested, not defined.

Banaras is a place of contrasts. Shadows and light. Movement and stillness. Trying to draw every person or every structure would have taken away from what I actually *felt* there.

So I focused on light - how it hit the steps, how it reflected in the Ganga, how it softened everything around it.

![<span class="hi">बनारसी सुबह</span> · Subah](/img/gallery/water (13).jpg)

![<span class="hi">बनारसी धूप</span> · Dhoop](/img/gallery/water (15).jpg)

I painted the same ghat at three different times of day, and each painting felt like a different city. The steps don't change. The river doesn't change. But the light is the entire story.

## Letting the painting *flow*

Watercolor naturally helped here. It doesn't demand perfection - it allows interpretation. (I've written separately about [how I approach a watercolor portrait, step by step](/blog/watercolor-portrait-guide.html) - the same flow-over-control idea, applied to faces instead of landscapes.)

There were moments where the paint spread in unexpected ways. Earlier in my practice, I would have tried to "fix" it. But that day, I let it be. And somehow, those uncontrolled moments made the painting more honest.

> Banaras teaches you to slow down - not just in life, but even in art. To observe more, control less, and trust the process.

I think that's what watercolor is about too.

## The *oil* attempt

I came back to Banaras a few months later with oils, and tried to paint a sadhu blessing pilgrims at Manikarnika Ghat. Same place, totally different medium, totally different feeling.

![<span class="hi">बनारसी दर्शन</span> · Banarasi Darshan - oil on canvas. Banaras translated into a slower, heavier medium.](/img/gallery/oil (4).jpg)

Oil rewards a different kind of patience. Watercolor wants you to commit and move on; oil wants you to come back tomorrow. Both are right answers to the same place.

## More than *just a painting*

By the time I finished the watercolors, they didn't feel like paintings of a location. They felt like *memories* - of the heat of the steps under my legs, the smell of incense at sunrise, the sound of bells before I ever opened my eyes that morning.

That, I think, is what plein-air painting is for. Not to make a record. To make a souvenir of an interior state.

If you're ever in Banaras, take a sketchbook. It doesn't need to be good. The act of sitting still and looking is the whole point.
