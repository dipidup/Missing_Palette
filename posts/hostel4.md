I did not arrive at BIT Mesra to become a painter. I arrived there to become an engineer. Things turned out differently, and most of why they turned out differently can be traced back to a single building - Hostel 4.

I had painted before college. As a kid in Ranchi, watercolours and a borrowed brush were how I spent afternoons. But it was the kind of painting that families humour - "*arre, kitna acchha banata hai*" - and then move on. It was not a thing one took seriously. School was a thing one took seriously. Marks. JEE. Engineering. The path was already drawn.

So in 2010 I packed a suitcase and went to Mesra to study mechanical engineering. The first painting I did in college was on the back of an answer sheet during a free hour in the second week.

## The room as a *studio*

Hostel 4 was nothing special as buildings go. Cement walls. Three-bed rooms. A common bathroom at the end of the corridor. The fan rattled. The tubelight flickered. None of that mattered.

What mattered was that the room was *mine* - the first space in my life that I did not have to share with parents or siblings or rules about where the paint could go. The first night I unpacked, I taped a sheet of cartridge paper to the wall above my bed, the way I had seen artists do in films. Within a week the wall behind my desk had four sheets pinned up. By the end of the first semester, the walls were full.

![A page from one of the sketchbooks - corridor of Hostel 4, drawn in the half-hour before a 9 a.m. class.](/img/gallery/sketch (1a).jpg)

Roommates came and went. Some were curious about the paintings. Most were tolerant in the way young men are tolerant of each other's strangeness - a shrug, a "*theek hai yaar*", and back to their own lives. One of them, Saurabh, used to bring me chai at two in the morning when he saw the light still on. He didn't ask what I was doing. He just put the cup on the desk and went back to bed.

## Sketchbooks *everywhere*

The mess hall. The library. The bus to town. The back row of every elective I disliked. I drew on whatever paper was available - the back of physics notes, the margins of practical files, Reynolds 045 ballpoint on whatever surface would take ink.

I think I filled six full sketchbooks across four years. Three of them are still in a trunk at my parents' house in Ranchi. The other three I lost - one to a flood in the room during monsoon, two to friends who borrowed them and never quite returned them. I am not bitter about the lost ones. They served their purpose by being made.

> The discipline of drawing every day for four years was not a plan. It was a side-effect of being bored in the right way.

That last part matters. I was not *trying* to become a painter. I was trying to get through fluid mechanics, and drawing was what I did instead. The seriousness came later. At the time, it was an escape valve. The fact that the escape valve happened to be the actual point of my life - that took me a few more years to understand.

## The *professor* who saw it

Halfway through second year I gifted a watercolour of the BIT Mesra campus to a senior physics professor I admired - Dr. Abhas Mitra, formerly of BARC. I gave it because I had nothing else to give him. He looked at it for a long time and said, "*Beta, this is not a hobby. You should know that.*"

He later wrote about me, calling me "a painter of professional caliber." It is one of the most generous things anyone has said about my work. At the time, twenty-year-old me did not know what to do with the comment. I shelved it. Years later I went back to it and realised it had been the first time someone with a long view of life had told me what I already half-knew but did not have permission to say out loud.

That permission is what most young artists are waiting for. Sometimes it comes from a teacher. Sometimes from a stranger. In my case it came from a physicist.

## What the *hostel* taught me

Years later, when I started teaching watercolour to others, I noticed I was drawing on lessons I had learned in Hostel 4 without realising they were lessons. Things like:

- **Make space** - even a corner of a shared room is enough. Studios are romantic but not required.
- **Don't wait for the right paper** - the back of an answer sheet works. The right paper will come; the practice should not wait for it.
- **Show your work to one person** - not the whole world, not Instagram. One person whose taste you trust. The feedback loop matters more than the audience size.
- **Treat boredom as a gift** - the most productive years of my life were the ones with the least to do.

## The painting *itself*

The watercolour at the top of this post - the one I gave Dr. Mitra a copy of - I painted it after I had already graduated. From memory, in a small flat in Ranchi, the year I started taking painting seriously as a career. I wanted to record the campus the way I had carried it inside me, not the way it was on a Google Maps photo.

I painted the buildings slightly warmer than they were. The trees taller. The light a little more golden. Memory does this on its own. I just followed it with a brush.

If you spent your own four years somewhere that turned you into something - a different city, a hostel, a flat above a shop, a friend's spare room - you know what I mean. The building is not the building anymore. It is a container for who you became inside it.

That is what Hostel 4 is for me. And that is why, of all the places I have painted, that one took the longest to start and the shortest to finish.
