Lead paragraph — this is the opening line, set in larger italic type. Use it to set the tone of the post in one breath.

This is a regular paragraph. Use these for the bulk of your writing. You can add bold emphasis sparingly, or italic when you mean it. Links inside paragraphs look like this — ochre, with a soft underline that warms to crimson on hover.

Keep paragraphs short. Two to four sentences each. White space is a feature, not a bug.

This is a section heading

Section headings use H2 tags and get a small dash automatically prepended in the styling. The italic emphasis renders in crimson.

Try to make headings feel like a thought, not a label. "Letting the painting flow" reads better than "Painting techniques."

This is a sub-heading

H3 is for sub-points within a section. Use sparingly — if you find yourself reaching for H3 a lot, the post probably wants to be split into more H2s instead.

Lists, when they help

Use bullet lists for genuinely list-like content. Don't bulletize prose just because you can.

  • Lead with the bold — the bolded phrase is a kind of mini-heading for the bullet
  • Then explain — the rest of the bullet is the supporting sentence
  • Keep them parallel — same shape across all bullets reads cleaner

A blockquote, when needed

For a sentence you want to give extra weight, use a blockquote. They're set in display serif and italic, with an ochre left border.

This is a pull-quote. Use it for the one sentence in the post you most want a reader to remember.

Don't use blockquotes for every other sentence. They lose their power that way.

Final thought

End with one closing paragraph. Pull the thread of the post together. Don't summarize — readers can scroll up. Instead, leave them with the one thing you want them to take with them.
